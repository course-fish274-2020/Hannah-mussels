Mussels and Sea Stars

The Data

This project seeks to compare the abundance of sea star Pisaster ochraceus and local mussels species in rocky intertidal zones across the US West coast. This project focuses on abundance data before and after a breakout of Sea Star Wasting Syndrome (SSWS).

Packages required : Tidyverse and Knitr

The data set used is from Sea Star Wasting Syndrome impacts to intertidal communities via mass mortality of a keystone predator

The relationship between sea stars and mussels

The sea star Pisaster ochraceus is a key stone predator for intertidal communities. As a keystone predator these sea stars keep populations of competitively dominant species (mussels) in check. Some advantages of their role in intertidal communities include:

  • Reducing competition for space
  • Protecting populations of producers (i.e kelp) and their consumers
  • Maintaining biodiversity

Sea Star Wasting Syndrome (SSWS)

Though not much is known about the causes of SSWS, the disease can be fatal to sea stars and has been known to cause mass mortality. The disease begins with lesions and eventually deterioration of the sea star’s body, ending in death.

Conclusion

  • After the occurrence of Sea Star Wasting Disease, average Pisaster density decreased while average Mussel percent cover increased.
  • This is most likely the result of Pisaster’s role as a keystone predator, without them mussels are left to out compete other species for space
  • Some regions ,such as Southern California, saw a decrease in mussel cover post SSWS. This could be the results of regional environmental or biological factors.
  • More research should be done to study the effect of SSWS on both Pisaster and other members of the rocky intertidal community.
